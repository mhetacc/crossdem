Il processo di Bologna inizia ad accompagnare la maggior parte dei sistemi per l'educazione
alta dell'Europa. Inizia con la Declarazione de Sorbonne, che si ha scritto solo per l'Italia,

Il numero di signatari continua a crescere, con 40 paesi a partire dalla conferenza

simbolizza l'importanza del processo come un processo genuinamente pan-europeano.
La Bologna è stata sviluppata e elaborata da 1999 con further conferenze interministeriali
in Praga nel 2001, e in Berlina nel settembre 2003. Le preparazioni sono ora all'interno
della prossima conferenza in Bergen, in Norvegi, nel maio 2005. Il processo è essenzialmente
interministeriali e, da inizio, è andato fuori dalla Unione Europea e le sue strutture
e è andato avanti molto più velocemente che i processi dell'Europa hanno inizialmente

per questo processo. Dunque, il processo è costruito sui trattamenti intergovernmentali

Quali sono gli essenziali del processo? L'obiettivo è di creare un'area europeana
per l'educazione alta dell'Europa nel 2010, per che i sistemi per l'educazione alta dell'Europa

nel mercato globale rapidamente sviluppato. La velocità con la quale il progresso è stato fatto

che l'Europa ha fatto verso gli obiettivi simili ma più limitati negli ultimi 20 anni.

higher education system? Underlying a number of the policy imperatives is the UK government
target for 50% of the generation leaving school to enter higher education, but with no commensurate

Some of the government's proposed solutions, most already being applied, are already having
a detrimental effect on the sector and on our members. One of the principal new policies
to achieve the student numbers target in the UK is to shift the concept of higher education
and of the degree itself. Just as countries across Europe are struggling with the three

which for many countries across Europe mean a shortening of the length of study to a minimum
of three years for bachelors and two years for masters degrees, the UK, which already

of many foundation courses will be judged by how readily they can be extended to a full

foundation degrees will be final qualifications. Many commentators fear that the government's
proposed expansion of higher education will be achieved by a significant devaluing of
the product, which is an offer to many young people. At the European level, inter-ministerial

it's notable that a few other European countries, including the Czech Republic, are following
the UK down the two-year route. Meanwhile at masters level, the UK model for masters
degrees is based mainly on one-year programmes, in contrast to the Bologna prescription of
two years, and this too could be a source of future conflict with our Bologna partners.
The fear of potential devaluing of qualifications is justified by the government's expectation
that an increasing proportion of higher education will be delivered in local intermediate
colleges. As universities become more closely linked with colleges offering intermediate
qualifications, we are likely to see a shift in the boundary between further education
and higher education, and the development of networks of feeder institutions around
major universities on lines closer to the US model. While this has potential advantages
in extending access and in opening up new opportunities for our members who teach
in colleges, there can be little doubt that in the government's view a lowering of the
unit price per student is the key determinant. In the same spirit, the government is seeking
to differentiate universities to create a hierarchy of institutions, elite institutions and
universities, which are actively funded to do a full research programme, a governmental
response to limited funds and burgeoning demand. It will be extremely hard, if not impossible,
for other institutions to break into the Charmed Circle. The crucial link between teaching
and research and scholarship will be broken. Many students will be taught in institutions
where research is at best a marginal activity. It will mean a diminution of the opportunities
many academics have to pursue their research interests, raising fundamental questions about
the nature of the academic's role, and reducing the role of academic staff in a way which
I suspect would be simply unacceptable in many European universities outside the UK.
Already the hierarchy is ossifying into self-selected bands of institutions headed by the Russell
Group of Universities, a would-be Ivy League, in the seat of most research funding. As well
as simply calling for more research funding to match the levels recommended by the OECD,
unions in the UK have called for more fluid responses. These could include encouraging
departments and groups of staff to do research regardless of the institution they're in, fostering
relations between institutions and the imaginative use of consortia. Also, while we attach great
importance to the overriding principle of public sector funding and values, in practice
diversity of funding sources is welcome, subject to appropriate protections.
This brings me to another theme in the UK system which may be familiar to you in Italy.
It's clear that the proportion of funding for higher education and research from the state
is and will continue to shrink, and funding from non-public sources, including corporate
finance, tuition fees and project funding, is expected to grow. One of the key planks
of government policy in the UK, tuition fees paid by students, is one of the most divisive

the fees policy, has been to argue for increased public investment in higher education and
research, but also to seek to ensure that the terms on which private funding is deployed

In the UK, institutions are also moving into increased reliance on short-term and part-time
contracts for their staff, and to teaching only appointments. The increasing availability
of postgraduate students without grants or other sources of income has opened up a new
and cheap form of academic labour, as well as an employment group who unions find it
difficult to recruit or to represent. Once again, these trends are clearly cost driven.
They represent a challenge to academic freedom, employment rights and the quality of the education
on offer. Quality is already taking on a new significance in institutions where students
are customers with new rights and expectations, and with the power to challenge what they
perceive as poor quality teaching or support for their learning. One institutional response
which must be a matter of concern is the upward drift in the UK of the level of passes
and of the grade of degree obtained. Quality assurance systems and the capacity of institutions
in different countries to depend on one another's awards will become steadily more important,
as will the maintenance of employer confidence in awards. At institutional level, robust
and transparent systems of quality assurance, built on peer review and rigorous internal processes,
must be developed to provide the essential protections backed by national and international
systems which verify the integrity of those systems. In the UK the quality assurance agency
fulfils this function. This in turn depends on institutions and academics having the time
and other resources to sustain these quality assurance systems. But contrary to what the
advocates of the market will argue, the philosophy built on the customer relations marketplace
mentality cannot sustain quality in higher education. Bologna can and must play an essential
positive role in insisting that quality assurance mechanisms do their job effectively.
The European Network for Quality Assurance, referred to above, is a European network of public
authorities concerned with quality universities and other institutions. Here, as elsewhere
at European level, intergovernmental and interinstitutional structures marginalise the unions.
The organisations representing the academics were most directly responsible for the maintenance,
delivery and verification of quality. Employer pressure towards decentralised bargaining,
moving away from national or regional or institutional level, and even with the possibility
of individual contracts with staff, all pose a new challenge to our national bargaining systems.
These pressures would place extra burdens on institutions and threaten the fragmentation
of the higher education system itself, making it more opaque to potential students, employers
and other customers, and will accelerate hierarchies of esteem among institutions and foster
inequity and a climate of competition, leading to a pursuit of the lowest common denominator
in salaries and conditions, except, as we've seen in recent years, for football-style
transfer packages to attract academic stars. Equally serious for us in the trade unions,
these trends will cause major organisational and representational problems for the academic
unions as they struggle to deal with more bargaining units and the need for cadres of
dedicated officials or active members to cope with the new bargaining levels.
Until very recently the title and concept of a university was jealously guarded in the
UK, where it required a royal charter to grant the university title. Then in the early
90s the polytechnics as a group became universities by an act of parliament and since then a
number of individual institutions have joined their ranks after meeting prescribed basic
criteria. But the global marketplace wishes to reprocess the image of the university
and see it as a brand which can be attached to forms of provision dissociated from a physical
entity or an institution with any of the characteristics which we would recognise as
defining a university. The University of the Future may well be a website, a network of
electronic resources or an in-house company training programme. Private corporations,
particularly in the USA and in East Asia, are increasingly applying the term university
to their own in-house training operations. One of the UK's largest employers, the National
Health Service, is setting up its own NHS university which will be from the outset one of our
biggest universities, operating through regional consortia with a number of conventional universities
as well as building links to intermediate and school level studies. Such developments
will undoubtedly reach new students or customers in innovative and user-friendly ways.
Their impact on the traditional university and the offers they make remains to be seen.
However, these developments present enormous challenges for staff and their unions and
for the general public in understanding and using such new forms of learning and transmission
of knowledge. The new managerial culture in institutions has already had a significant
effect on institutional culture. It continues to act in a corrosive way, for example,
taking income generation and publishing as key criteria for assessing staff performance.
The fact that this trend has been driven from within the institutions themselves makes obsolete
the belief that institutional autonomy in itself is a protection for academic staff.

and must continue to have a key role in the education of teachers both initially and throughout their
careers. These issues should bind the teachers unions together and enable them to present a

In UK unions as in other sectors of education creaking structures depend on an ageing cohort
of academic staff in full-time tenured posts able to maintain a balance of teaching, research
and scholarship as well as administrative responsibilities who carry the traditional values of the
university system and who are often the ones challenging threats to those values as individuals

terms while many are on casualised contracts and thus far more vulnerable to the whims of the employer.
The UK unions in higher education also rely heavily on the generation now in its last decade of work

when they look at the way future academic careers are likely to unfold they may take the view that
union activism or even union membership is not good for the health of your career.
This is a major challenge for unions in education. The higher education unions and the UK

The issues raised by Bologna and the global marketplace in higher education run through them as a subtext.

in the global market. Both the EU and the Bologna strands of policy are driving us towards a model which should retain
and may even enhance some of the valued collegial qualities of higher education and research within Europe
